I study what happens when governments automate. Automatic decision-making systems, AI, large language models and blockchain are reshaping the public sector. My research asks who benefits and who bears the costs.

My central concern is equity. I approach it through three lenses. At the level of frontline public servants, I examine how artificial intelligence transforms the nature of work, the skills required, and professional identity.1 Turning to the people organizations serve, I ask how digital channels shape citizens' access to and engagement with government2 and how race and deservingness shape the allocation of administrative burdens.5 Looking at the technologies themselves: how large language models encode historical inequities into contemporary urban policy,3 how blockchain restructures the efficiency and accountability of judicial systems,4 and whether generative AI replicates these patterns in welfare eligibility decisions.6

I am a Research Assistant at the Center for Technology in Government (CTG UAlbany), working under Prof. Mila Gasco-Hernandez.

You can find me presenting at dg.o 2026 (Omaha, NE, June 1–4) and PMRC 2026 (Washington, DC, June 6).

Prior to joining Albany, I completed my first year of doctoral study at Arizona State University. I studied as an MPhil student in Innovation, Policy and Entrepreneurship at the Hong Kong University of Science and Technology (Guangzhou). My undergraduate study was at Zhejiang University, where I majored in International Politics.
